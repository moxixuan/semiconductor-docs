  6. 5.4.1 Before analyzing the sample, perform a series of analyses using a source of oxygen of which the purity has been previously determined. Only after three consecutive analyses have been run, indicating the known purity, should you proceed with testing. (See Note 7.)

If the gas sample in the buret is below the zero mark raise the leveling bottle #1 so that its “water” level is even atmosphere so as to raise the “water” level in the buret to exactly zero (Diagram B, Fig. #1).

Transfer at least 25 to 30 mL of test solution from the left-hand leveling bottle #2 into the right-hand leveling bottle #l, through the copper coil bottle #3, and the gas buret while, at the same time, gently rocking and tapping the center bottle (Diagram B, Fig. #4).   9. 5.4.4 Turn the stopcock off and raise the leveling bottle #l so that its liquid level is the same as the liquid level inside the upper portion of the gas buret.   11. 5.5.1 Copper coils should be added to the test bottle as required to keep the bottles completely full. The gas buret should be kept clean with a strong detergent solution to eliminate drops of liquid that might hang up in the gas space and give incorrect purity readings. No readings should be taken when the space above the liquid level of the gas buret has any liquid drops hanging in it, as this will give an erroneous purity reading.
  12. 5.5.2 The solution must be replaced occasionally as it becomes exhausted. The necessity of replacement may be determined when the color begins to turn green, and by the increased length of shaking time required to get a minimum acceptable purity from the oxygen in a cylinder of known purity. Suspect an exhausted solution if analysis of a cylinder of known purity does not read the correct purity.
